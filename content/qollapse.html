Qollapse

By Samir Rajesh

Thesis & Code ↗

Qollapse is an autonomous simulation that explores identity as something relational, unstable, and historically shaped rather than fixed or pre-defined. A single “Seeker” agent exists in a superposition of possible relational roles, collapsing into a specific identity only through observation and interaction with others. Over time, patterns of behaviour, attachment, hesitation, and stability emerge—not by script, but through accumulated encounters inside a living world.


The project was developed as my MSc Creative Computing thesis at the University of the Arts London. It combines agent-based simulation, interpersonal psychology, and quantum-inspired decision models to examine how identity forms through repeated social encounters rather than static internal traits.


Four classical agents—Mother, Sage, Peer, and Lover—are modelled using a six-dimensional interpersonal trait space. Their behaviours are stable but mutable, drifting gradually as they interact with others. In contrast, the Seeker’s identity is represented as a quantum-inspired superposition over four relational roles: Child, Disciple, Friend, and Partner. Observation by others rotates this potential identity state, while interaction causes it to collapse into a specific role for that moment.


These dynamics are implemented in Unity using a lightweight C# port of MicroQiskit to handle state vectors, rotations, entanglement-like coupling, and probabilistic collapse. The simulation runs continuously, generating unscripted behaviour shaped by spatial layout, day–night cycles, and the order in which encounters occur. Identity emerges as a trajectory rather than an endpoint.


Qollapse includes an integrated narrative layer that exposes internal processes—observations, role collapses, actions, and trait shifts—as a live textual log. Long-run simulations are logged to CSV and analysed offline using Python, producing trait-drift plots, circumplex trajectories, collapse matrices, and spatial density maps. These visualisations reveal consistent global attractors alongside role-specific identity paths: secure, oscillatory, hesitant, or highly regulated.


Rather than presenting identity as something an agent “has,” Qollapse treats identity as something that happens: a process enacted through relation, context, and time. The project functions both as a research prototype and as an open-ended world—an environment designed to think with, observe, and potentially break.
